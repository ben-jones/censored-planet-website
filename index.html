What is Censored Planet?

Censored Planet’s goals are to discover what governments censor, how they restrict access, and how decisions about censorship vary across regions, countries, and time. Existing approaches cannot perform censorship measurements at a sufficient scale to answer these questions; worse, users must perform these measurements on-site, putting people at risk. We avoid these problems by building Censored Planet on top of advance network inference channels that allow researchers to measure connectivity between arbitrary endpoints, which satisfy certain basic requirements, from almost anywhere. By using these network inference channels such as idle scans, that don’t require any access to any of the machines that connectivity is tested to or from, we can shed light on entirely unexplored areas of the Internet. Our framework makes significant contributions to the current state of Internet censorship detection by trying to solve the following challenges:

  • Broad coverage from many representative vantage points. We could not rely on deploying dedi- cated measurement agents, nor could we rely on recruitment of individual activist users, neither of which provide scale or broad coverage. In contrast to previous studies, we seek to perform measurements of censorship from as many vantage points as possible that don’t belong to any end users (e.g. routers).
  • Cross-layer measurements. Censors often change the methods that they use to implement censorship, so studying filtering at multiple layers of the protocol stack is important. We focus on TCP/IP, DNS-based filtering and Http layer, which lie at the foundation of Internet connectivity and are three common protocols that governments use to implement censorship.
  • Coordinated snapshots. Many political scientists want to study how censorship varies across regions within a country or across countries. As a result, we need better ways to conduct measurements to the same set of destinations from different vantage points within roughly the same time period. Doing so requires designing fast, lightweight measurement techniques that still yield accurate information.
  • Minimal (or zero) risk to citizens. Measuring Internet censorship carries an existential risk for citizens. Although we are not aware of any country where measuring censorship is explicitly illegal, measuring censorship may still subject a citizen to unknown existential risks (e.g., increased surveillance). Thus we measure only from infrastructure vantage points, which helps to preclude any links between our measurements and users

Getting Involved

We want to involve a self-motivated student to help us with our Censored Planet project. We are a group of academics who have experience with successfully mentoring students over the past years. This year, we are hoping to involve a highly motivated coder with a strong web development background.

Working for this project is rewarding for many reasons:

  • Being involved in a project that matters to humanity because we expose violations of free speech. This project will help the public understand the alarming influence of governments’ agendas in blocking information in different parts of the world.
  • Working with and analyzing large amounts of data. In many future projects, you might need skills to deal with big data. We have a great deal of experience in working with such datasets.
  • Getting a feeling for interdisciplinary research. Internet censorship does not just concern computer science and researchers from many different fields are working on it. In addition to software development, you will get a feeling for interdisciplinary research in general and for this field in particular.
 

How to Get Involved:
